HR's Next Socio-Technical Moment

Headless agents will not just automate workplace tasks. They will change how work is delegated, measured, and governed. That makes them a people problem as much as a technology problem.

This essay extends the agentic systems thread from my earlier writing into HR Tech and organisational design. I work at Culture Amp, but this is personal thinking based only on public sources and my own views.

The Next User Might Not Be Human

A quiet assumption sits underneath most workplace software: a human logs in, navigates a user interface, interprets the information on screen, and decides what to do next. That assumption is starting to weaken. The emerging pattern is not just chatbots embedded inside existing products. It is software becoming addressable by agents through APIs, tools, commands, and protocols.

Salesforce recently described this shift bluntly with Headless 360: capabilities that previously lived behind a browser are being exposed so agents can call them directly. Anthropic's Model Context Protocol points in the same direction: a more standard way for AI systems to connect to the places where work data already lives. The underlying direction is simple. The surface changes. The work system remains.

That is why the phrase "headless agents" is more interesting than it first sounds. A recent AI Daily Brief episode on headless agents put useful language around a pattern that is already emerging. The important part is not that agents can run without a visible UI. It is that they can become participants in workflows that used to require people to click, copy, summarise, route, remind, and reconcile. They can act across systems. They can run on a schedule. They can inherit permissions. They can leave audit trails. They can become part of the operating fabric of the company.

HR Tech should pay attention to this. Not because every HR workflow should be automated, but because HR has always been concerned with the design of work: how people are managed, how performance is understood, how trust is built or broken, and how organisations adapt when the nature of work changes.

The Stale Comparison Still Contains a Useful Lesson

It is tempting to reach for the Industrial Revolution analogy here. It is also easy to make that analogy so broad that it stops meaning anything. "AI is the new steam engine" is not a very useful sentence.

A more precise historical lesson comes from socio-technical systems thinking. In the 1950s, researchers associated with the Tavistock tradition studied how new mining technology changed work in British coal mines. The technical change was real, but the deeper finding was that productivity and worker experience depended on the joint design of the technical system and the social system. Optimising the machinery while damaging the human work structure could make the whole system worse.

That is the useful comparison for agentic work. Headless agents are not just tools that individual employees choose to use. They are changes to the work system. They alter who does what, where judgment is applied, how handoffs happen, what gets measured, and who is accountable when something goes wrong.

If companies treat agents as a purely technical deployment, they will miss the harder question: what social system are those agents entering, and how will that system need to change around them?

From Automation to Informating

Another useful lens comes from Shoshana Zuboff's In the Age of the Smart Machine, where she coined the term informating to capture a distinction that still matters: digital systems do not only automate work. They also make work newly visible. They create data about how work happens.

Agents intensify that pattern. A headless agent does not only complete a task. It may record what was delegated, which systems were touched, which instructions were ambiguous, which decisions required escalation, which human overrides occurred, and where the organisation relied on undocumented knowledge. In other words, agents may become a new source of operational telemetry about the real shape of work.

That can be valuable. It can reveal bottlenecks, unclear ownership, policy friction, duplicated effort, and recurring management failure. It can also become creepy very quickly. The same visibility that helps a company improve work can also become surveillance, pressure, and a quiet collapse of autonomy.

This is where HR cannot sit downstream from the technology decision. If agentic systems produce new data about work, organisations need a point of view on what should be measured, what should not be measured, who can see it, how it should be interpreted, and when it should trigger human intervention.

The Risk Is Taylorism with APIs

The cautionary version of this future is not hard to imagine. Work gets decomposed into smaller tasks. Tasks are routed to humans or agents. Every interaction becomes measurable. Managers get dashboards. Employees get nudges. Productivity becomes legible in ways that feel objective, even when the underlying model of work is incomplete.

That is not a new story. Scientific management tried to turn work into measurable units more than a century ago. More recently, platform companies showed how software can become management: allocating work, scoring performance, shaping incentives, and constraining human discretion. Agentic white-collar work could bring a softer version of algorithmic management into ordinary organisations.

The danger is not that agents will be useless. The danger is that they will be useful enough to spread before companies have developed the organisational discipline to use them well.

A badly designed agentic workplace could improve throughput while degrading trust. It could reduce admin while increasing ambiguity about accountability. It could help managers write better messages while making employees wonder whether anyone is actually listening. It could make work faster and thinner at the same time.

People Science Belongs in the Architecture

This is why I think People Science is a more interesting lens than generic AI productivity discourse. Culture Amp describes People Science as deeply embedded in how it understands work, combining organisational psychology, research, data, and product practice to help organisations create better cultures and better results. Publicly, Culture Amp's AI positioning is similarly grounded in AI plus people science, not AI as a replacement for human judgment.

That distinction matters. If agents become part of the work system, the question is not only whether they can complete tasks. It is whether they improve the conditions around human performance: clarity, confidence, fairness, trust, learning, and the quality of management decisions.

Culture Amp's public writing on the Performance Culture Quadrant makes a useful point here: sustainable high performance is not simply an individual trait. It is a collective property shaped by the conditions around people. That idea should travel directly into agentic work.

If an agent helps one employee move faster but creates confusion for the team, the system may not be healthier. If an agent automates manager follow-up but weakens trust because people cannot tell what is automated and what is human, the system may not be better. If an agent reduces toil while improving clarity, feedback, and follow-through, then it may genuinely increase organisational capacity.

What HR Tech May Need to Become

The obvious HR Tech response to AI is to add AI features to existing workflows: summarise comments, draft reviews, recommend actions, coach managers, answer policy questions. Those features matter, but they are probably not the endpoint. Workday's public framing of an Agent System of Record is a signal that the category is already moving beyond AI assistance toward governance of agents themselves.

The larger shift is that HR systems may need to become legible to agents while remaining accountable to people. That means more than exposing APIs. It means making the organisation's people context available in ways that are permissioned, interpretable, and bounded by good judgment.

Imagine a manager-support agent acting after an engagement survey. A shallow version summarises comments and drafts a message. A better version understands team context, highlights risks, proposes follow-up rituals, suggests 1:1 prompts, reminds the manager to close the loop, and escalates when sensitive issues require human care. The best version also knows what not to do. It does not automate empathy. It does not infer more than the data supports. It does not turn private employee sentiment into a productivity weapon.

That is a different product philosophy. HR Tech becomes less like a dashboard people occasionally visit and more like a people-context layer inside the operating system of work. Not a source of automated decisions, but a source of scientifically grounded signals, guardrails, and interventions that help humans and agents act better together.

A Prediction

The companies that win with agents will not simply be the ones that automate the most tasks. They will be the ones that redesign work as a socio-technical system: using agents to increase capacity while preserving trust, autonomy, learning, fairness, and accountability.

For HR, that means the discipline cannot be confined to policy, engagement surveys, performance cycles, or change communications after the fact. HR will need to be involved in the architecture of agentic work itself: what agents are allowed to do, how work is delegated, how human judgment is preserved, how employee experience is measured, and how the organisation learns from the data these systems create.

Headless agents may enter the company through engineering, sales, support, finance, or operations. But if they reshape how work actually happens, they eventually become HR's problem too. More optimistically, they become HR's opportunity: a chance to make the future of work not just faster, but better designed.

Agents make work faster. HR makes it better designed.
