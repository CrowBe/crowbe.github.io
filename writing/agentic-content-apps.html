Agentic Content Apps — A New Pattern

Personal AI agents are moving well beyond chat. If they can handle the work of fetching and structuring data, what does that mean for the apps built around them?

Something Is Shifting

A year ago, the phrase "personal AI agent" described something fairly specific: a chat window, a model waiting for instructions, an assistant that could answer questions and draft emails reasonably well. That picture is changing, and changing quickly. The agents emerging now can browse the web on your behalf, authenticate with services, run scheduled tasks, call tools, and chain those actions together into workflows that run without you watching. They are becoming, in a more literal sense than the name previously implied, agents — things that act.

Sequoia's 2025 AI 50 report noted that AI agents were moving meaningfully beyond chat interfaces — from answering questions to completing tasks autonomously. Others have started asking what the future of AI looks like when it isn't a conversation at all. I think this trajectory raises a question that doesn't yet have a settled answer: if a personal agent can handle the work of fetching, filtering, and structuring content from across the web, what does that mean for the apps that currently do that work?

This article is my attempt to think through one possible answer — a pattern I've been calling agentic content apps, and that I've been building toward with ScrolLess. I'm not announcing a new paradigm. I'm describing something I can see the edge of, and thinking out loud about what it might become.

The Problem Apps Carry

Look at the codebase of almost any content app — a news reader, a social aggregator, a personal dashboard — and you'll find that a significant portion of it has nothing to do with the actual experience the app is trying to deliver. OAuth flows, API rate limit handling, token refresh logic, schema mappings that need updating every time a platform changes its response format: a great deal of engineering effort goes into simply getting the data, before a single line of thought can be spent on what to do with it.

This wasn't always the shape of things. In the early web, apps owned their data because they were the source of it — you were putting information into them, not pulling it out of somewhere else. Then came the platform era, and suddenly every interesting piece of user data lived behind someone else's API and terms of service. Apps adapted by becoming integration layers: brokers of authentication, orchestrators of API calls, engines of synchronisation. The experience became almost incidental to the plumbing required to reach it.

There's a less-visible cost that falls on users. When an app owns your integrations, it also owns, in a practical sense, your data. Switching to a competing app means starting from scratch. Deleting your account means losing your history. Your preferences, your reading patterns, your saved items — all of it lives in someone else's database, subject to their retention decisions and business continuity. This arrangement is so normalised that it barely registers as a choice. But it is a choice, embedded in the architecture, and not the only one available. Scholars studying data sovereignty in the age of big tech have been pointing to this structural problem for years — the question is whether the tooling now exists to address it differently.

The Inversion I'm Exploring

The pattern that's been occupying my thinking goes roughly like this: what if a personal AI agent took over the integration work entirely — authenticating with platforms, fetching content, normalising it into a common format — and the app simply received the result? The agent handles the plumbing. The app handles the experience. And the data, rather than being held in a backend somewhere, lives on the user's own device.

In this model, the agent runs on a schedule. It knows which sources you follow, what you care about, and where to put the output. It structures everything into a consistent schema and delivers it to your device. The app — any app that speaks the right format — reads from that local store and renders. Nothing more. The relationship between integration complexity and application complexity, which currently maps almost one-to-one, comes apart.

The inversion matters for two reasons, and they apply to different people. For developers, integration complexity moves out of the application entirely — the app's job becomes rendering well-structured data, which is a much smaller and more focused problem. For users, the arrangement with their data changes: the agent gathers on their behalf, the device holds the result, and no intermediary is custodian of their content as a condition of using the service. Switch apps, and your history follows. Delete the app, and your data is still there.

What Changes

The practical difference between a traditional content app and the pattern I'm describing comes down to where responsibility lives at each step:

Concern Traditional App Agentic Content App
Data gathering App connects directly to platforms Agent handles all fetching
Integrations App maintains API clients per platform Managed in the user's agent layer — new platforms added without touching the app
Data ownership Held by the app's backend Stored on the user's device
Authentication App brokers OAuth per service Agent authenticates independently
App's core job Integration + presentation Presentation only
Portability Data locked to the app Data portable across apps

A Sketch of How It Works

The technical shape of this, in its simplest form: the agent runs on a schedule, reads a configuration of which sources to check, authenticates and fetches from each, structures the results into a common payload, encrypts that payload using the device's public key, and delivers it to the device via a lightweight relay. The relay passes it through without reading it — the payload is ciphertext by the time it arrives. The device decrypts, stores locally, and the app reads from there.

To keep this reliable rather than unpredictable, the agent works with deterministic workflows rather than free-form reasoning. MCP (Model Context Protocol) tools wrap each source's logic with typed inputs and outputs — named, repeatable operations that run the same way every time. The intelligence is in deciding what to gather and how to prioritise it. The mechanics of fetching are structured, not improvised. The architecture of Article 3 in this series covers this in detail.

Beyond Content Feeds

Content feeds are the natural starting point for this kind of thinking — the integration cost is obvious and the user benefit of portability is immediate. But the pattern isn't limited to feeds. Anywhere an app currently has to maintain integrations with external sources to assemble a personalised view, the same logic applies.

Budgeting apps are one example. The open banking integrations they require — the bank-specific API clients, the maintenance overhead as institutions change formats — represent exactly the kind of plumbing that an agent could absorb. The app would receive normalised transaction data and focus on categorisation, visualisation, and insight. The research dashboard is another: a researcher tracking a topic across academic databases, news sources, and social feeds today has to move between disconnected tools or build custom pipelines. An agent could monitor all those sources, normalise the results, and surface them through a single interface that has no knowledge of how any individual source works.

The common thread is that the app gets narrower in scope and deeper in quality. Rather than doing thirty things adequately — integrations, sync, auth, storage, rendering — it does one thing well: making the data useful. That's a different kind of software from what most content apps are today, and I think a more interesting one to build.

An Early Exploration: ScrolLess

ScrolLess is my working experiment in this direction — a unified social and news feed aggregator built around this architecture. An external AI agent handles scraping the platforms I follow, encrypts the payload using the device's public key, and delivers it through a relay server that is deliberately blind to the content. The app — a Preact PWA — reads from IndexedDB and renders. The server holds nothing; it routes encrypted ciphertext and that's all it can do.

The project is still in active development, and I want to be honest about that. The core delivery pipeline is working — agent scraping, encrypted relay, device-side storage — and the architectural principle (no direct platform integrations in the app itself) is structural from the start, not something to be added later. The full range of sources, the preferences UI, and the multi-device sync tier are still being built. ScrolLess is less a finished product than a probe into what this pattern feels like in practice.

Apps stop being data owners. They become tools again.
