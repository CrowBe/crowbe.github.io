When Agents Join the Team, Managers Need to Tend the System

As AI agents join the enterprise workforce, individual contributors will need managers who make the system clearer, healthier, and more human.

This essay continues the thread from Why Agents Are a People Problem, Not Just a Technology Deployment. I work at Culture Amp, but this is personal thinking based on public sources and my own experience as an individual contributor.

Customer Zero for Agentic Work

One of the phrases I like at Culture Amp is Customer Zero. The idea is that if we are building products that help other companies work better, we should be willing to experience the change ourselves first, and learn from our own friction rather than only from customer feedback after the fact. That framing makes the current shift toward agentic systems of work feel less abstract. For a SaaS company building AI into its products and internal workflows, the transition is not only something to analyse from the outside; it is something we live inside, and we are becoming one of the workplaces changed by it.

I am not a manager myself, so this is not a prescription from that side of the table. It is the view from an individual contributor looking up and sideways, thinking about what people like me will need from managers as agents become part of how work gets done.

Management Was Never Just Task Assignment

The best managers I have worked with were not valuable because they moved tickets around. They were valuable because they improved the system around the work — clarifying what mattered, noticing when priorities were incoherent, creating space for honest feedback, and helping people grow without turning development into theatre. That view is not especially novel. Culture Amp's writing on the State of the Manager describes a modern shift away from supervision and toward empowerment, mentorship, alignment, development, and feedback, while Google's Project Oxygen research lands in similar territory: good managers coach, empower, communicate, support development, and avoid micromanagement. The agentic workplace does not invent the idea that managers should be more than task assigners; it raises the cost of forgetting it.

Agents Make Execution Cheaper, Not Context Easier

A useful agent can draft, summarise, transform, search, reconcile, and sometimes act across systems. That makes some forms of execution cheaper and faster, but it does not automatically make the work system clearer. In fact, more delegation paths can introduce new ambiguity: which work should go to an agent and which should stay human, when an agent is acting on behalf of a person versus a team, who owns the result, and whether a shortcut actually improved the outcome or simply made the activity less visible.

These are not only tooling questions; they are management questions, sitting in the space between strategy, team norms, accountability, trust, and judgement. The more agents can do, the more important it becomes for managers to make the surrounding context explicit.

What Individual Contributors Will Need

From an individual contributor's seat, the first need is clarity. If agents increase throughput, they can also increase noise, and the most useful manager in that environment is not the one who finds ten more things to automate, but the one who keeps explaining what matters, what does not, and why. Closely related is autonomy: as more execution moves to agents, individual contributors need to keep the parts of the work where judgement matters, not just the residual decisions agents could not make. Healthy autonomy means people can shape how agents are used in their work, challenge or override their outputs, and are not silently routed around when they have a better answer. The risk is the opposite, where agents quietly narrow the space in which humans get to think.

The next need is growth. Culture Amp's guide to employee development makes the basic point that meaningful development is central to engagement and retention, and that becomes sharper when agents enter the picture. A good agentic workplace should not quietly deskill people by hiding the interesting parts of the work; it should help them move up the value chain toward better judgement, better craft, and better systems thinking. Underneath all of that sits trust — not vague trust in AI, but trust in the local work system: that automation will not become surveillance by another name, that humans can challenge outputs, that mistakes will be handled as learning rather than blame, and that managers will notice the human cost of a workflow, not only its apparent efficiency.

The Manager as Systems Gardener

This is where the gardener metaphor works for me. A gardener does not grow the plant by pulling on it; they tend the conditions — soil, light, water, space, timing, pruning, protection from the things that choke growth — to create an environment where growth is more likely. In an agentic workplace, the garden is the work system, where people, agents, tools, rituals, goals, feedback loops, and incentives all interact. A manager cannot force every outcome and probably should not try, but they can tend the conditions that make better outcomes more likely.

In practice, that means paying attention to whether agents are genuinely removing toil or quietly hiding complexity, whether people are gaining real autonomy or being routed around, whether feedback loops are getting faster or merely noisier, and whether junior people still have opportunities to learn the work deeply. Atlassian's Team Health Monitor is a useful practical example of this kind of thinking, treating team health as something to inspect and improve deliberately rather than as a soft by-product of shipping. Agentic work needs a similar habit, where the relevant question is not only whether the system delivered more output, but whether it became healthier to work in.

A More Human Version of Management

There is a pessimistic version of agentic work where managers become operators of dashboards, employees become sources of signals, and agents become a polite layer over command and control. That future is possible but not inevitable. The more optimistic version is that agents absorb some of the coordination drag that has crowded out better management, handling the repetitive drafts, reminders, summaries, handoffs, and checks that consume attention without always deserving it. Managers then have more room for the work that was always more valuable: sensemaking, coaching, prioritising, noticing weak signals, resolving ambiguity, and helping people grow.

Microsoft's 2025 Work Trend Index puts numbers to this. Of AI users surveyed, 66 percent report spending more time on high-value work, and 86 percent treat agent output as a starting point rather than a final answer. The constraint, the same report finds, is not individual readiness — it is organisational readiness. Organisational factors account for more than twice the AI impact of individual factors, yet only 19 percent of knowledge workers currently operate in what the report calls Frontier conditions, where individual capability and organisational readiness reinforce each other. As the researchers put it: in many cases, people are ready — the systems around them are not. That gap is exactly where management sits.

That is what I would want from managers in a Customer Zero transition. Not certainty about every tool, not blind enthusiasm, and not resistance dressed up as prudence, but a willingness to experiment with agents while protecting the human system around the work.

If agents take on more of the doing, great managers do not matter less. They need to tend the system.
