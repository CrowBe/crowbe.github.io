When Agents Join the Team, Managers Matter Differently

As AI agents join the enterprise workforce, individual contributors will need managers who make the system clearer, healthier, and more human.

This essay continues the thread from HR's Next Socio-Technical Moment. I work at Culture Amp, but this is personal thinking based on public sources and my own experience as an individual contributor.

Customer Zero for Agentic Work

One of the phrases I like at Culture Amp is Customer Zero. The idea is simple: if we are building products that help other companies work better, we should be willing to experience the change ourselves first. We should learn from our own friction, not only from customer feedback after the fact.

That makes the current shift toward agentic systems of work feel less abstract. For a SaaS company building AI into its products and internal workflows, the transition is not only something to analyse from the outside. It is something we live inside. We are not just asking what agentic work might mean for customers. We are also becoming one of the workplaces changed by it.

I am not a manager, so this is not a prescription from that side of the table. It is the view from an individual contributor looking up and sideways, asking what people like me will need from managers as agents become part of how work gets done.

Management Was Never Just Task Assignment

The best managers I have worked with were not valuable because they moved tickets around. They were valuable because they improved the system around the work. They clarified what mattered. They noticed when priorities were incoherent. They created space for honest feedback. They helped people grow without turning development into theatre. They made the team easier to work in.

That view is not especially novel. Culture Amp's public writing on the State of the Manager describes a modern shift away from mere supervision and toward empowerment, mentorship, alignment, development, and feedback. Its Manager Effectiveness 180 methodology defines effective management around inspiring, motivating, supporting, caring for people, and still achieving business outcomes. Google's Project Oxygen research, referenced in that methodology, lands in similar territory: good managers coach, empower, communicate, support development, and avoid micromanagement.

In other words, the agentic workplace does not invent the idea that managers should be more than task assigners. It raises the cost of forgetting it.

Agents Make Execution Cheaper, Not Context Easier

A useful agent can draft, summarise, transform, search, reconcile, and sometimes act across systems. That matters. It makes some forms of execution cheaper and faster. But it does not automatically make the work system clearer.

In fact, it may do the opposite. More delegation paths mean more questions. Which work should go to an agent? Which work should stay human? When is an agent acting on behalf of a person, a team, or the company? Who owns the result? How do we know whether the shortcut improved the outcome or merely made the activity less visible?

Those are not only tooling questions. They are management questions. They sit in the space between strategy, team norms, accountability, trust, and judgement. The more agents can do, the more important it becomes for managers to make the surrounding context explicit.

What Individual Contributors Will Need

From an individual contributor's seat, the first need is clarity. If agents increase throughput, they can also increase noise. The most useful manager in that environment is not the one who finds ten more things to automate. It is the one who keeps explaining what matters, what does not, and why.

The second need is autonomy. As more execution moves to agents, individual contributors need to keep the parts of the work where judgement matters, not just the residual decisions agents could not make. Healthy autonomy means people can shape how agents are used in their work, can challenge or override their outputs, and are not silently routed around when they have a better answer. The risk is the opposite: agents quietly narrowing the space where humans get to think.

The third need is growth. Culture Amp's public guide to employee development makes the basic point that meaningful development is central to engagement and retention. That becomes sharper when agents enter the picture. A good agentic workplace should not quietly deskill people by hiding the interesting parts of the work. It should help people move up the value chain: better judgement, better craft, better systems thinking, better taste.

The fourth need is trust. Not vague trust in AI, but trust in the local work system: that automation will not become surveillance by another name; that humans can challenge outputs; that mistakes will be handled as learning rather than blame; and that managers will notice the human cost of a workflow, not only its apparent efficiency.

The Manager as Systems Gardener

This is where the gardener metaphor works for me. A gardener does not grow the plant by pulling on it. They tend the conditions: soil, light, water, space, timing, pruning, and protection from things that choke growth. The point is not control. The point is creating an environment where growth is more likely.

In an agentic workplace, the garden is the work system. People, agents, tools, rituals, goals, feedback loops, and incentives all interact. A manager cannot make every outcome happen by force, and probably should not try. But they can tend the conditions under which better outcomes become more likely.

That means asking different questions. Are agents removing toil, or just hiding complexity? Are people gaining more autonomy, or being routed around? Are feedback loops getting faster, or just noisier? Are junior people still getting opportunities to learn the work deeply? Are we using agents to strengthen relationships with customers and each other, or to avoid the uncomfortable human parts of the job?

Atlassian's Team Health Monitor is a useful practical example of this kind of thinking. It treats team health as something to inspect and improve deliberately, not as a soft by-product of shipping. Agentic work needs a similar habit. The question is not only whether the system delivered more output. It is whether the system became healthier to work in.

A More Human Version of Management

There is a pessimistic version of agentic work where managers become operators of dashboards, employees become sources of signals, and agents become a polite layer over command and control. That future is possible, but it is not inevitable.

The more optimistic version is that agents absorb some of the coordination drag that has crowded out better management. They handle the repetitive drafts, reminders, summaries, handoffs, and checks that consume attention without always deserving it. Managers then have more room for the work that was always more valuable: sensemaking, coaching, prioritising, noticing weak signals, resolving ambiguity, and helping people grow.

That is what I would want from managers in a Customer Zero transition. Not certainty about every tool. Not blind enthusiasm. Not resistance dressed up as prudence. Something more useful: a willingness to experiment with agents while protecting the human system around the work.

If agents take on more of the doing, great managers do not matter less. They matter differently.
